## Restating the Fully Formulated Problem - Alec

### Objective
This project's primary customer is Lani Solar, LLC (LaniEV). LaniEV is developing a line of standardized retrofit conversion kits for low-speed electric vehicles (LSVs), personal transport vehicles (PVTs),  and golf cars. These retrofit kits replace the legacy lead-acid battery with a LiFePO₄ (Lithium Iron Phosphate) battery bank integrated with a self-charging roof solar panel. The goal is to commercialize the conversion kits for entire fleet conversions. Secondary stakeholders include Tennessee Technological University (TTU), as the owner of the control golf car used for the project, and as the ensuring agency for documented safety, reliability, and performance data. 

Legacy golf cars continue to utilize lead-acid battery systems due to their well-documented electrical performance and broad availability. These systems present constraints such as low energy density, volume constraints, short lifecycle, load limits, maintenance requirements, and environmental concerns. These limitations increase cost and decrease sustainability for large-scale applications. Alternative power systems, such as LiFePO₄ batteries integrated with solar charging, could potentially address the limitations inherent in the lead-acid batteries. The growing interest in alternative systems demands formal documentation for conversion processes. The lack of validation prevents large-scale migration of LSV, PVT, and golf car platforms to alternative energy systems.
 
This project will address the lack of technical validation through meticulous examination and documentation of the conversion process and will also establish a validated framework for implementing the standardized retrofit kit. The development includes initial state characterization of the existing lead-acid system, formalization of the conversion methodology, post-conversion performance evaluation, and a quantified analysis of environmental and economic impact. The Tennessee Tech University Club Car will serve as the control platform for this process. Baseline electrical and functional characteristics of the legacy system shall be documented prior to conversion. Following installation of the LiFePO₄ battery and solar charging systems, the vehicle shall undergo structured testing to quantify energy efficiency, power delivery, charging behavior, and operational performance. The resulting documentation shall provide the necessary metrics required for the retrofit kit to be commercialized.

### Constraints:
- **NEC Article 690:**
    - **Origin:** The National Electrical Code
    - **Impact:** Sets standards for solar photovoltaic systems and applies to the electrical integration of the solar panel, charge controller, and associated wiring. 

- **29 CFR 1910:**
    - **Origin:** OSHA Regulations
    - **Impact:** Governs safe practices during live testing and assembly to protect team members working with electrical systems, including lockout/tagout (1910.147) and electrical safety-related work practices (1910 Subpart S).

- **UL Certification:**
    - **Origin:** Underwriters Laboratories standards
    - **Impact:** Components such as charge controllers, DC-DC converters, battery management systems (BMS), and wiring assemblies may require compliance with relevant UL standards (e.g., UL 1741 for inverters and controllers, UL 1973 for stationary batteries, UL 2580 for electric vehicle battery systems) [5]. Use of UL-listed components reduces liability exposure and supports insurability.

- **SAE Standards (J2344, J1772):**
    - **Origin:** SAE International
    - **Impact:** Provides guidelines for EV battery system safety (J2344) and conductive charging interfaces (J1772), ensuring predictable and safe operation under environmental and electrical conditions.

- **IEEE Standards (1547, grounding, insulation coordination):**
    - **Origin:** Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers
    - **Impact:** Governs grounding, insulation, and interconnection of distributed energy resources to ensure safe integration with electrical systems and prevent faults.

- **NFPA 70 / 70E / 855:**
    - **Origin:** National Fire Protection Association
    - **Impact:** NFPA 70 (NEC) governs electrical installations. NFPA 70E addresses electrical safety in the workplace and may apply during testing and live diagnostics. NFPA 855 may apply if energy storage systems exceed certain capacity thresholds.

- **FMVSS (Low-Speed Vehicle Standards):**
    - **Origin:** Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards
    - **Impact:** Governs lighting, braking, and electrical safety integration for LSVs intended for on-road use, ensuring safe operation under applicable vehicle regulations.

- **EPA and Environmental Regulations:**
    - **Origin:** Environmental Protection Agency and state-level rules
    - **Impact:** Requires compliant battery disposal, recycling, and hazardous material handling for lithium-based battery systems.

- **ANSI Standards (Z535, electrical safety labeling):**
    - **Origin:** American National Standards Institute
    - **Impact:** Governs electrical safety labeling, signage, and equipment performance to ensure clear hazard communication and standardization of high-voltage warnings.

### Hardware Block Diagram

Block diagrams are an excellent way to provide an overarching understanding of a system and the relationships among its individual components. Generally, block diagrams draw from visual modeling languages like the Universal Modeling Language (UML). Each block represents a subsystem, and each connection indicates a relationship between the connected blocks. Typically, the relationship in a system diagram denotes an input-output interaction.

In the block diagram, each subsystem should be depicted by a single block. For each block, there should be a brief explanation of its functional expectations and associated constraints. Similarly, each connection should have a concise description of the relationship it represents, including the nature of the connection (such as power, analog signal, serial communication, or wireless communication) and any relevant constraints.

The end result should present a comprehensive view of a well-defined system, delegating all atomic responsibilities necessary to accomplish the project scope to their respective subsystems.


### Operational Flow Chart

Similar to a block diagram, the flow chart aims to specify the system, but from the user's point of view rather than illustrating the arrangement of each subsystem. It outlines the steps a user needs to perform to use the device and the screens/interfaces they will encounter. A diagram should be drawn to represent this process. Each step should be represented in the diagram to visually depict the sequence of actions and corresponding screens/interfaces the user will encounter while using the device.
